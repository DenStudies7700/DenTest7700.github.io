Life During The 1920s




Great Excitement when WW1 Ended. Old countries were abolished, new ones were formed, boundaries were redrawn, international organizations were established, and many new and old ideologies took a firm hold in people's minds.

Prohibition in the United States was a nationwide constitutional ban on the production, importation, transportation, and sale of alcoholic beverages from 1920 to 1933. This decreased the arrests for public drunkers, decreased the crime rate and improved the efficiency of industrial workers.

Spanish flu was carried after the war with the soliders. The virus quicky got spread among the canadian population so that made alot of places such as churches and schools closed. Spanish flu killed around 50,000 canadians and 21m people died in the worldwide.

Wheat on the Prairries
From 1925-1929 the prairies provinces enjoyed huge wheat crops. Recovering Europe could now aford buying canadian wheat. With the extra profits farmers made, they bought more mechanized farms.

Foreign Investment
Prior to WW1, the biggest investors in canadian industry were the British. However during the war, British investment decreased amd was replaced by American's Investment.

Mining
Rich deposits of copper, nickel lead and zink were formed acorss canadian shield. There was greater demands for metals in the manufactoring industery.

Oil and Gas
Automobiles created a great demand for oil and gas. The search for "Black Gold" was on.

Pulp and Paper
Giant American newspaper companies needed more paper. As a result, across the country the canadian forest industry thrived. However, Many forests were cleared out.

Dance
One of the most famous dances of the 1920s was the Charleston. Many of the younger generation loved the dance, while the older generation saw it as dangerous.


Life During The 1930s




Few Canadians owned stocks. However, America and Canada's stock were both tied and Canada exported many prouducts to United States. Many companies who bought Canadian producsts were now bankrupt and that made the Canadian economy start to suffer.

The Great Depression Starts
When the stock market crashed, it helped to trigger a decade of hardship for many people. The stock market was viewed as a trigger effect that helped to start the depression, but not the cause.

Overproduction and Overexpansion
One cause of the Great Depression was too many product had been produced and left unsold. Factories and other businesses had expanded beyond their means. People had less money to spend.

Canada's dependence on few Primary Products
Canada's economy relied too heavily on pulp and paper, mining, wheat and fish. When the demand for these products decreased around the world, the Canadian economy dropped.

Canada's Dependance on the U.S.A.
The Canadian economy was very closely tied to the American economy. When the stock market crashed and the depression started in the U.S.A. it inevitably hit canada hard.

Too Much Buying On Credit
Canadians encouraged to, "Buy now, pay later". Cars and home appliances were bought on credit and paid off using monthly payments. When people lost their jobs, they had no money to make monthly payments.

Too Much Buying Of Stocks on Credit
People bought stocks on credit and paid back their debts with profits made on stocks. However, if the stock value decreased, the shareholders had no money to pay back their creditors.

Music
Technological advacements also brought about the radio. As the cost of radios decreased more and more people bought them. The radio became a new form of entertainment. It was a good distraction for people from the hardships of life during depression too.

Games
The game Mahjong became one of the fads of the 1920s and 1930s. During the depression it served as a way for people to forget their problems and simply enjoy time with others.

BLACK BLIZZARD
The black blizzards started in the eastern states in 1930, affecting agriculture from Maine to Arkansas. By 1934, they had reached the Great Plains, stretching from North Dakota to Texas and from the Mississippi River Valley to the Rocky Mountains.
